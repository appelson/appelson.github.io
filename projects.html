Visualization Police Violence in Louisiana

The Visualizing Police Violence in Louisiana dashboard was launched in January 2024. The dashboard was created to enhance data accessibility and stewardship, amplify voices, and create accountability concerning policing in Louisiana. The dashboard launch event had 98 registrants and approximately 40 attendees. The dashboard launch also had a significant reach airing on multiple news channels, radio shows, and news articles. In total, the media coverage reached over 1,000,000 people.

Juvenile Crime In Louisiana, by the Numbers

The Juvenile Justice analysis supports the ACLU of Louisiana’s policy fight during the current Special Session on Crime. This analysis has been utilized by multiple organizations, including the Louisiana Center for Children's Rights.

Washington et al. V. Smith et al.

The lawsuit data analysis aims to demonstrate that the St. Tammany Parish Sheriff’s Office is discriminating against African Americans through their traffic stops. This research included a large-scale predictive analysis on the demographics of people stopped, their citations, and an Optical Character Recognition (OCR) of citation PDFs. We found that from January through November of 2023, Black individuals in St. Tammany Parish were 250% more likely to be stopped for alleged traffic violations than white individuals. While 15% of the Parish’s residents identify as Black, they account for 36% of the people stopped and 26% of those cited. We are still in the midst of this lawsuit.

The Advocate Media Analysis

The Advocate analysis examines how media coverage influences perceptions of crime in Louisiana. We analyzed every article from The Advocate from 2016 and found that out of 102,029 news articles, 27,799 (27%) were in the "police and crime" category. Coverage varied from 34% in 2016 to 18% in 2023, with fluctuations in between, including an increase to 24% in 2021.

One vs. Two-year Statute of Limitation Impact Factsheet

The Statute of Limitation Impact Factsheet challenges the narrative that shorter statutes of limitations result in more meritorious cases. Our analysis indicates that raising the statute of limitations in Louisiana for police action cases may reduce court caseloads and lead to more meritorious cases. We also uncovered that shorter statutes disproportionately affect Black communities. This analysis is critical for a potential Supreme Court case challenging the current statute of limitations in Louisiana, a leading justice issue in the state.
